**SCHOOLS ARE BIG BUSINESS**

For example, the school district of one typical Midwestern city of 100,000 population has a capital investment in its school plant of $9,315,924, employs a total personnel of 663, and spends each year approximately $3,410,177 for current operation. No single business firm in this city can match these figures. The same thing will hold true, with few exceptions, in communities of every size throughout our land.

It should follow, then, that merely from a business standpoint, the job of superintendent of schools is one of the biggest administrative jobs in the community. In some areas, the superintendent of schools is responsible for transporting more individuals each day than all public transportation services combined. He is often responsible for school lunchrooms serving more people than all the hotels and restaurants combined. He is always responsible for safeguarding the health and well-being of a larger segment of the population than ever falls at one time under the care of all the area's social agencies combined. When we add to such physical and material aspects a consideration of the enduring human values which we expect to evolve from the educational process, the importance of the responsibility resting upon the superintendent becomes even more apparent.

The Development of the New York State Normal School

BY FRANCIS J. CHENEY, CORTLAND NORMAL SCHOOL.

To most of you it is, without doubt, a familiar story I have to tell,
and yet may not be without profit to repeat it.

The normal school as an institution has existed less than 300 years.
The first organization for the training of teachers, of which we have any
account, believed to have been established by La Salle at Reims, in about
the year 1683. A few years after the efforts of La Salle to furnish an
opportunity to those who wished to prepare themselves for teaching,
Augustus Hermann Ranke established a teachers' class at Halle, in
Prussia. He provided free board for such poor students as stood in need
of assistance, and selected out of the whole number twelve who exhibited
the right basis of piety, knowledge, skill and desire for teaching, and
constituted them his "Seminarium Preceptorium," or teachers' Seminary.
These pupil teachers received instruction for two years in methods, and,
in return, promised to teach for three years in the institution after
nishing their course. So successful was this work, that it is said, hundreds
of teachers were attracted from all parts of Europe to observe and study
the organization, methods and spirit of the school.

Other classes for teachers were opened in other parts of Germany.
Courses of lectures on the best methods of teaching German and the
ancient languages became common in the universities and higher schools
of Germany. From Germany the system spread to other parts of Europe;
but the normal school idea, we understand it, made little headway until
the beginning of the nineteenth century.

It is a mooted question how far these efforts in Europe to train
teachers influenced the normal school idea in the United States. Some
contend that they had no influence upon us whatever, as the earliest
advocates of the normal school in this country knew little or nothing of
what had been done in Europe. Although the seed was planted here as

The normal schools that were established, subsequently, under general and special acts, were organized on the same plan. In 1891 a so-called scientific course was added, consisting of the same subjects as the advanced English, with the addition of two years of Latin and two years of either French, German or Greek.

In 1892, on the recommendation of the normal school principals, the elementary English course was abolished. The remaining three were continued until 1900, when the scientific course was dropped. The English course lengthened to four years, the classical course somewhat modified, and two two-year courses, English and classical, for high school graduates, were added.

Along with the unification of our educational departments has come another change resulting in a general reorganization of the normal schools in this State. They now consist of a training school, a model high school and a normal or professional school proper. The training school is made up of all the grades below the high school, in many instances including the kindergarten department.

In the high school there are three courses of study, each four years in length.

The first course is intended for those who wish to enter the normal or professional school, and is open to any resident of the State who wishes to prepare for teaching, and who does not reside in a district where a high school is already established.

The second prepares for admission to college, and the third for admission to schools of technology. These last two are open to students only who live within the limits of the corporation where the normal school which they attend is located.

Students, however, who wish to take the normal school preparatory course for admission to the normal can do so; instead of the first course mentioned.
